About India

Ancient India

India's recorded civilisation begins with the Indus Valley Civilisation (around 2500 BCE), one of the world's earliest urban cultures, centred on cities like Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. This was followed by the Vedic period, which laid the foundations of classical Indian thought, society, and religion. Major empires such as the Mauryas (Chandragupta Maurya, Ashoka) and Guptas (the so-called "Golden Age") unified large parts of the subcontinent and advanced science, mathematics, and the arts.

Medieval India

The medieval period saw regional kingdoms, the Delhi Sultanate, and later the Mughal Empire, which brought administrative reform, architecture (Taj Mahal, Red Fort), and a syncretic culture blending Indian, Persian, and Central Asian influences. Vijayanagara and Maratha powers in the south and west were major centres of resistance and regional governance.

Colonial Rule and the Freedom Struggle

European trading companies, principally the British East India Company, gradually expanded political control after the Battle of Plassey (1757). The Revolt of 1857 was the first large-scale uprising against Company rule, after which India came directly under the British Crown. The freedom movement that followed — led by the Indian National Congress, Mahatma Gandhi's campaigns of non-cooperation, civil disobedience, and Quit India, alongside revolutionary and armed movements including Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose's Indian National Army — culminated in independence on 15 August 1947.

Post-Independence

India adopted its Constitution on 26 January 1950, becoming a sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic. Since then, it has integrated princely states, conducted the world's largest democratic elections, built a diversified economy, and emerged as a major regional and global power, including becoming a recognised nuclear-capable state.

Physical Divisions

India is broadly divided into the Himalayan mountain system in the north, the Indo-Gangetic Plains, the Peninsular Plateau (including the Deccan Plateau), the Coastal Plains, the Thar Desert in the west, and Island groups — the Andaman & Nicobar Islands in the Bay of Bengal and Lakshadweep in the Arabian Sea.

Rivers and Climate

Major river systems include the Ganga, Brahmaputra, Indus, Godavari, Krishna, and Narmada, which support agriculture and population centres across the plains. India's climate is predominantly tropical monsoon, governed by the southwest and northeast monsoon winds, with significant regional variation from the cold Himalayan zones to the arid Thar Desert and humid coastal belts.

Strategic Geography

India shares land borders with Pakistan, China, Nepal, Bhutan, Bangladesh, and Myanmar, and maritime boundaries with Sri Lanka, Maldives, and Indonesia. Its location astride major Indian Ocean shipping lanes gives it considerable strategic importance, and border geography — high-altitude Himalayan terrain in the north, riverine and deltaic terrain in the east, and desert/marsh terrain in the west — directly shapes the deployment and role of the armed forces.

Constitutional Framework

India is governed by the Constitution of India, the longest written constitution in the world, which came into effect on 26 January 1950. It establishes India as a sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic with a parliamentary system of government and a federal structure with unitary features.

Structure of Government
  • Executive: President (head of state), Prime Minister and Council of Ministers (real executive authority), and state-level Governors and Chief Ministers.
  • Legislature: Bicameral Parliament — Lok Sabha (House of the People, directly elected) and Rajya Sabha (Council of States, representing the states).
  • Judiciary: An independent judiciary headed by the Supreme Court, with High Courts at the state level, responsible for upholding the Constitution and protecting fundamental rights.
Civil-Military Relations

The President of India is the Supreme Commander of the Armed Forces, while actual control rests with the civilian government through the Ministry of Defence. This principle of civilian control over the military is a foundational feature of Indian democracy and a topic frequently discussed in SSB interviews and Lecturette.

Overview

India has a mixed economy, combining private enterprise with public sector participation, and is among the largest economies in the world by GDP. Economic liberalisation since 1991 opened the economy to global trade and investment, driving sustained growth across services, manufacturing, and agriculture.

Key Sectors
  • Agriculture: Employs the largest share of the workforce; remains vital for food security and rural livelihoods.
  • Services: The largest contributor to GDP, led by information technology, financial services, and telecommunications.
  • Manufacturing: A growing sector supported by initiatives such as "Make in India," with increasing focus on defence and electronics manufacturing.
Defence-Economy Link

Defence indigenisation — through programmes like Atmanirbhar Bharat (self-reliance) in defence production, the Defence Acquisition Procedure, and growing domestic defence manufacturing — links India's economic policy directly to its military capability and is a frequently tested current affairs topic for defence exams.

Indian Army

The Indian Army traces its lineage to the armies of the East India Company, later reorganised as the British Indian Army after 1858, and became the Indian Army upon independence in 1947. It is the largest of the three services and is primarily responsible for land-based operations, defending India's borders, internal security support, and disaster relief. It is organised into Commands, Corps, Divisions, Brigades, and Regiments, with a mix of infantry, armoured, artillery, engineering, and support arms.

Indian Navy

The Indian Navy's origins go back to the Royal Indian Marine and later the Royal Indian Navy, renamed the Indian Navy after independence. It is responsible for safeguarding India's maritime borders, the Exclusive Economic Zone, sea lines of communication, and projecting power across the Indian Ocean Region. It operates surface ships, submarines, naval aviation, and is organised into the Western, Eastern, and Southern Naval Commands.

Indian Air Force

The Indian Air Force was established on 8 October 1932 as part of British India's air arm and adopted its current name and independent command structure after 1947. Its mandate is to secure Indian airspace, conduct air defence, offensive air operations, strategic airlift, and provide air support to the Army and Navy, along with humanitarian and disaster relief operations.

Integrated Defence Structure

All three services function under the Ministry of Defence, with the Chief of Defence Staff (CDS), created in 2019, providing single-point military advice to the government and driving jointness, theaterisation, and integration across the Army, Navy, and Air Force.

Origin

The Indian Coast Guard was established on 1 February 1977 under the Coast Guard Act, 1978, as an independent armed force, formed in response to the growing need to police India's maritime zones following the declaration of the Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ). It functions under the Ministry of Defence, distinct from the Navy, though it works in close coordination with it.

Role and Responsibilities
  • Enforcement of maritime laws within India's territorial waters and EEZ
  • Protection of offshore oil installations, fishing fleets, and marine ecology
  • Search and rescue operations at sea, including during cyclones and maritime disasters
  • Anti-smuggling, anti-piracy, and counter-infiltration operations along the coast
  • Assistance to fishermen and enforcement of coastal security after the 2008 Mumbai attacks led to a major expansion of coastal surveillance

The motto of the Indian Coast Guard, "Vayam Rakshamah" ("We Protect"), reflects its core mission of safeguarding India's vast coastline and maritime interests.

India's paramilitary and central police forces — officially termed Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF) — operate under the Ministry of Home Affairs (with the exception of the Assam Rifles, which is administratively under the Home Ministry but operationally controlled by the Army). They handle internal security, border guarding, counter-insurgency, and law-and-order support, distinct from the defence-focused Armed Forces.

Border Security Force (BSF) — Est. 1965

Formed after the 1965 India-Pakistan war exposed the need for a dedicated border-guarding force, BSF guards India's borders with Pakistan and Bangladesh during peacetime and supports the Army during war.

Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF) — Est. 1939 (as Crown Representative's Police)

India's largest CAPF, primarily responsible for internal security, counter-insurgency operations (notably in Left Wing Extremism-affected areas and Jammu & Kashmir), and law-and-order duties including election security.

Indo-Tibetan Border Police (ITBP) — Est. 1962

Raised after the 1962 India-China war, ITBP guards the Indo-China border in high-altitude Himalayan terrain and is trained in mountaineering and disaster response.

Sashastra Seema Bal (SSB) — Est. 1963

Originally raised as the Special Service Bureau after the 1962 war, it now guards the open borders with Nepal and Bhutan and conducts counter-insurgency and intelligence operations in border areas.

Central Industrial Security Force (CISF) — Est. 1969

Provides security to critical infrastructure such as airports, nuclear installations, major ports, and key government and industrial establishments across the country.

Assam Rifles — Est. 1835 (oldest paramilitary force)

India's oldest paramilitary force, originally raised as the Cachar Levy, it now guards the India-Myanmar border and conducts counter-insurgency operations in the Northeast, earning the title "Sentinels of the North-East."

National Security Guard (NSG)

A federal contingency force raised in 1984 for specialised counter-terrorism duties, including hostage rescue and anti-hijack operations, often called the "Black Cats."

Know Your Regiment

Regiments & Units

The Indian Army's fighting strength is organised into regiments, each with its own history, recruitment class, and battle honours. This section also covers Coast Guard and Central Armed Police Force units, along with their major gallantry awards.

Infantry regiments form the backbone of the Indian Army, responsible for holding ground, close-quarter combat, and offensive land operations. Most are "class" or "all-India, all-class" regiments raised from specific regions or communities, a system retained from the colonial era for unit cohesion. Each regiment is administered through a Regimental Centre that trains and feeds new recruits into its battalions.

  • The Grenadiers (raised 1778, Centre: Jabalpur) — 23 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. Among the oldest infantry regiments; holds the record for the most Param Vir Chakras of any single regiment — 3 (1965, 1971, and a further PVC for gallantry), alongside numerous Vir Chakras and Maha Vir Chakras.
  • Sikh Regiment (raised 1846, Centre: Ramgarh Cantt) — 20 regular + 3 TA + 3 RR battalions. Renowned for the defence of Saragarhi (1897) and Battle of Rezangla-area operations; has earned 2 Param Vir Chakras and the highest number of gallantry awards of any regiment over its history.
  • Kumaon Regiment (raised 1813, Centre: Ranikhet) — 20 regular + 2 TA + 3 RR battalions + Kumaon Scouts. Holds 2 Param Vir Chakras, including the Army's first-ever PVC (Major Som Nath Sharma, 1947) and the legendary stand at Rezang La (1962).
  • Maratha Light Infantry (raised 1768, Centre: Belgaum) — 22 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. One of the oldest light infantry regiments; known for speed and aggressive infantry tactics, with multiple Maha Vir Chakra and Vir Chakra recipients across wars.
  • Rajput Regiment (raised 1778, Centre: Fatehgarh) — 20 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. A highly decorated regiment with a strong showing in the 1965 and 1971 wars and in counter-insurgency operations; multiple Maha Vir Chakras to its credit.
  • Rajputana Rifles (raised 1775, Centre: Delhi Cantt) — 19 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. Among the most decorated regiments in Indian Army history, with a large tally of gallantry awards spanning both World Wars and post-independence conflicts.
  • Jat Regiment (raised 1795, Centre: Bareilly) — 22 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. Known for its role in 1965, 1971, and Kargil; recipient of multiple Maha Vir Chakras and Vir Chakras.
  • Madras Regiment (raised 1758, Centre: Wellington) — 21 regular + 3 TA + 4 RR battalions. The oldest regiment of the Indian Army; has served in nearly every major campaign since the 18th century, with a long list of battle honours.
  • Punjab Regiment (raised 1761, Centre: Ramgarh Cantt) — 21 regular + 3 TA + 4 RR battalions. One of the senior-most regiments; motto "Sthal wa Jal" ("By Land and Sea"); extensive battle honours from both World Wars and post-independence campaigns.
  • Dogra Regiment (raised 1877, Centre: Ayodhya) — 20 regular + 4 TA + 4 RR battalions + Dogra Scouts. Recruits primarily from the Jammu hills; distinguished service in high-altitude warfare and counter-insurgency.
  • Garhwal Rifles (raised 1887, Centre: Lansdowne) — 21 regular + 2 TA + 3 RR battalions. Known for mountain warfare; Naik Darwan Singh Negi and Gabar Singh Negi earned the Victoria Cross in World War I, and the regiment has since added Param Vir Chakra-level valour in independent India's conflicts.
  • Mahar Regiment (raised 1941, Centre: Saugor) — 21 regular + 3 TA + 3 RR battalions. Now an all-class regiment; notable for the heroics of Second Lieutenant Arun Khetarpal's contemporaries and for its role in 1971; closely associated with Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's advocacy for its re-raising.
  • Bihar Regiment (raised 1941, Centre: Danapur) — 22 regular + 2 TA + 4 RR battalions. Played a key role in the 1971 war's eastern theatre operations.
  • Assam Regiment (raised 1941, Centre: Shillong) — 15 regular + 5 TA + 3 RR battalions. Specialises in the terrain and counter-insurgency conditions of the Northeast; distinguished itself in the 1962 Battle of Walong. (Arunachal Scouts is affiliated.)
  • Naga Regiment (raised 1970, Centre: Ranikhet) — 3 regular + 1 TA + 1 RR battalions. The youngest infantry regiment, focused on Northeast operations and counter-insurgency.
  • Jammu & Kashmir Rifles (raised 1821, Centre: Jabalpur) — 23 regular + 3 TA + 3 RR battalions. Specialises in mountain and high-altitude warfare in J&K; holds 2 Param Vir Chakras. (Ladakh Scouts is affiliated.)
  • Jammu & Kashmir Light Infantry (JAK LI) (raised 1947/re-org 1972, Centre: Rangreth, Srinagar) — 15 regular + 3 TA battalions. Locally recruited regiment focused on counter-insurgency and border defence within J&K.
  • Brigade of the Guards (raised 1949, Centre: Kamptee) — 22 regular + 2 TA + 1 RR battalions. India's first all-class, all-India infantry regiment (now mechanised); includes 14 Guards, whose Lance Naik Albert Ekka won the Param Vir Chakra in 1971.
  • Sikh Light Infantry (raised 1944, Centre: Fatehgarh) — 19 regular + 3 TA + 3 RR battalions. Distinct from the Sikh Regiment; multiple gallantry awards including a Param Vir Chakra-equivalent legacy through its battalions.
  • Ladakh Scouts (raised 1963, Centre: Leh) — 5 battalions. "Snow Warriors" — specialist high-altitude regiment recruited from Ladakhi tribes; played a decisive role in Kargil 1999.
  • Arunachal Scouts (raised 2010, Centre: Pasighat) — 2 battalions. Locally recruited scout regiment for the Arunachal Pradesh / LAC sector.
  • Sikkim Scouts (raised 2013, Centre: Lucknow) — 2 battalions. Locally recruited scout regiment for the Sikkim / North Sikkim LAC sector.

Battalion strength figures cover Regular + Territorial Army (TA) + Rashtriya Rifles (RR) battalions affiliated to each regiment. The Indian Army's infantry fights through roughly 380+ infantry battalions across these regiments.

The Gorkha Rifles are regiments recruiting Nepali-origin Gorkha soldiers (both Indian and Nepalese domiciled), continuing a martial tradition dating back to the Anglo-Nepalese War of 1814-16. Seven Gorkha Rifles regiments serve in the Indian Army today, specialising in mountain warfare, close-quarter combat with the iconic khukri, and high-altitude operations.

Battalion Strength of Each Gorkha Regiment
  • 1 Gorkha Rifles (The Malaun Regiment) — raised 1815, Centre: Subathu — 6 battalions.
  • 3 Gorkha Rifles — raised 1815, Centre: Varanasi — 5 battalions + 1 TA.
  • 4 Gorkha Rifles — raised 1857, Centre: Sabathu — 5 battalions.
  • 5 Gorkha Rifles (Frontier Force) — raised 1858, Centre: Shillong — 6 battalions.
  • 8 Gorkha Rifles — raised 1824, Centre: Shillong — 5 battalions.
  • 9 Gorkha Rifles — raised 1817, Centre: Varanasi — 5 battalions + 1 TA.
  • 11 Gorkha Rifles — re-raised 1948, Centre: Lucknow — 6 battalions + 1 TA. (Sikkim Scouts affiliated)

Total Gorkha strength: ~38 regular + TA + RR battalions, with roughly 32,000 Gorkha soldiers serving.

Awards

As a collective, Gorkha Rifles regiments have earned 3 Param Vir Chakras — one each by 1st Gorkha Rifles (The Malaun Regiment), 8th Gorkha Rifles, and 11th Gorkha Rifles — alongside numerous Maha Vir Chakras, Vir Chakras, and a strong World War-era Victoria Cross legacy. Subedar Major and Honorary Captain Bhanbhagta Gurung, Naik Agansing Rai, and other Gorkha soldiers were decorated for extraordinary valour across both World Wars.

Armoured Corps

The Armoured Corps operates India's tank regiments (T-72, T-90, Arjun) for armoured warfare, breakthrough operations, and rapid manoeuvre. The corps fields approximately 67 armoured regiments (a "regiment" in the Armoured Corps is the equivalent of an infantry battalion, ~45–50 tanks each). Its regiments carry "Horse" or "Lancers" titles from their cavalry origins.

  • 17 Poona Horse — India's most decorated armoured regiment; holds 2 Param Vir Chakras (Lt. Col. Ardeshir Tarapore, 1965; Second Lieutenant Arun Khetarpal, 1971) for its role in the Battles of Phillora and Basantar.
  • Hodson's Horse, Central India Horse, Deccan Horse, Scinde Horse, Skinner's Horse, 7 Light Cavalry, 18 Cavalry, 20 Lancers — Historic cavalry regiments with extensive battle honours from both World Wars and post-independence conflicts.
  • President's Body Guard (PBG) — The senior-most regiment of the Indian Army (raised 1773), the ceremonial mounted bodyguard of the President of India; doubles as an operational airborne tank troop.
Mechanised Infantry Regiment

A relatively newer arm — the Mechanised Infantry Regiment (raised 1979, Centre: Ahmednagar) — fields 27 battalions operating BMP-2 "Sarath" infantry combat vehicles, fighting alongside armour in mechanised formations and combining infantry firepower with battlefield mobility.

  • Regiment of Artillery (lineage from 1827, Centre: Nashik & Hyderabad) — Around 280+ artillery regiments (field, medium, self-propelled, missile and rocket). Provides indirect fire support with field guns, howitzers, and rocket systems (Bofors, Dhanush, K9 Vajra, Pinaka, BrahMos); its motto "Sarvatra Izzat O Iqbal" ("Everywhere with Honour and Glory") reflects its role across every battle the Army has fought.
  • Corps of Engineers — Three Sapper groups: Madras Sappers (Bengaluru), Bengal Sappers (Roorkee), Bombay Sappers (Pune), fielding around 60+ engineer regiments. Builds and breaches obstacles, bridges, and fortifications; Second Lieutenant Rama Raghoba Rane of the Corps of Engineers won the Param Vir Chakra in 1948.
  • Corps of Signals (raised 1911, Centre: Jabalpur & Goa) — Manages military communications, including modern network-centric and electronic warfare systems.
  • Army Aviation Corps (raised 1986, Centre: Nashik) — Operates helicopters (Cheetah, Cheetal, Rudra, ALH Dhruv, Apache) for reconnaissance, casualty evacuation, and close support to ground troops.
  • Army Air Defence (raised 1994 as separate corps, Centre: Gopalpur) — Protects ground formations from aerial threats using missile (Akash, QRSAM) and gun-based (ZSU Shilka, ZU-23) air defence systems.

The Parachute Regiment (raised 1945, reorganised 1952, Centre: Bengaluru) fields ~15 battalions in total — broadly 9 Para (Special Forces) battalions plus 5 Para (Airborne) battalions plus 1 Rashtriya Rifles battalion plus 2 Territorial Army battalions. It houses both Airborne (Para) battalions, which conduct parachute insertion and rapid-deployment operations, and Para Special Forces (Para SF) battalions, India's elite special operations units trained for deep-strike, counter-terrorism, and unconventional warfare. Para SF selection is among the toughest in the Indian Army.

Awards

The regiment carries a strong gallantry record — Captain Manoj Kumar Pandey of 1 Para (Special Forces) was awarded the Param Vir Chakra posthumously during the Kargil War (1999) for the capture of Khalubar.

The Indian Navy does not follow the Army's regimental system; instead it is organised into branches by specialisation and Commands by geography, with personnel serving aboard ships, submarines, and aircraft rather than in fixed land-based units.

Branches
  • Executive Branch — Seamanship, navigation, gunnery, and command of ships and submarines; the General Service stream that produces the Navy's seagoing commanding officers.
  • Engineering Branch — Maintains and operates marine engineering systems, propulsion, and weapons platforms aboard ships and submarines.
  • Electrical Branch — Handles weapon systems, sensors, communication, and electronic warfare equipment.
  • Education Branch — Manages training, meteorology, and oceanography support for naval operations.
  • Naval Aviation — Operates carrier-borne and shore-based fighters, helicopters, and maritime patrol aircraft (e.g. MiG-29K, P-8I).
  • Submarine Arm — Operates India's conventional (Kalvari, Sindhughosh class) and nuclear-powered (Arihant class) submarines for undersea warfare and strategic deterrence.
  • Marine Commando Force (MARCOS) — The Navy's special forces, trained for amphibious assault, maritime counter-terrorism, and clandestine operations.
Commands
  • Western Naval Command (Mumbai) — Guards the Arabian Sea coast and western maritime approaches.
  • Eastern Naval Command (Visakhapatnam) — Guards the Bay of Bengal and is home to India's strategic submarine fleet.
  • Southern Naval Command (Kochi) — The Navy's principal training command.
Awards

No Param Vir Chakra has been awarded to the Navy to date, but naval personnel have earned numerous Maha Vir Chakras and Vir Chakras, most notably during Operation Trident and Operation Python in the 1971 war, when naval missile boats struck Karachi harbour. Vice Admiral and submariner gallantry has also been recognised with Kirti Chakras and Shaurya Chakras in peacetime operations.

The Indian Air Force is organised into operational and functional Commands rather than regiments, with fighter, transport, and helicopter squadrons as its basic fighting units.

Commands
  • Western Air Command (Delhi) — Covers the Pakistan border and the National Capital Region's air defence.
  • Eastern Air Command (Shillong) — Covers the China border in the Northeast.
  • Central Air Command (Allahabad/Prayagraj) — Covers the central sector along the Tibet border.
  • South Western Air Command (Gandhinagar) — Covers the Rajasthan and Gujarat sectors bordering Pakistan.
  • Southern Air Command (Thiruvananthapuram) — Covers peninsular India and training establishments.
  • Training Command (Bengaluru) and Maintenance Command (Nagpur) — Functional commands for pilot/airman training and aircraft maintenance respectively.
Garud Commando Force

The IAF's special forces unit, trained for airbase protection, search and rescue in hostile zones, and direct action alongside Army and Navy special forces.

Awards

Flying Officer Nirmal Jit Singh Sekhon remains the only Air Force recipient of the Param Vir Chakra, awarded posthumously for single-handedly engaging six enemy aircraft over Srinagar during the 1971 war. IAF pilots have also earned numerous Vir Chakras and Maha Vir Chakras across the 1965, 1971, and Kargil conflicts, including Kargil's air operations under Operation Safed Sagar.

The Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF) — BSF, CRPF, ITBP, SSB, CISF, and the Assam Rifles — are organised into battalions and sectors rather than traditional Army-style regiments, but each force maintains its own distinct role, training, and decorated history.

  • Border Security Force (BSF) — Guards the Pakistan and Bangladesh borders; its personnel have received Ashok Chakras, Kirti Chakras, and Shaurya Chakras for anti-infiltration and counter-terrorism operations, including extensive gallantry during the Kargil War in a support role.
  • Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF) — India's largest CAPF; its COBRA (Commando Battalion for Resolute Action) units specialise in jungle and Left Wing Extremism operations and have earned numerous Shaurya Chakras and Police Medals for Gallantry.
  • Indo-Tibetan Border Police (ITBP) — Guards the high-altitude Indo-China border; decorated for mountaineering rescues and border-area operations, including several Shaurya Chakras.
  • Sashastra Seema Bal (SSB) — Guards the Nepal and Bhutan borders; recognised for intelligence and counter-insurgency operations in border districts.
  • Central Industrial Security Force (CISF) — Secures airports, nuclear and industrial installations; commended for thwarting numerous security incidents at critical infrastructure.
  • Assam Rifles — India's oldest paramilitary force (1835); holds a long list of gallantry awards for Northeast counter-insurgency, including multiple Ashok Chakras and Kirti Chakras over its 185+ year history.
  • National Security Guard (NSG) — India's premier counter-terrorism force; NSG commandos were awarded Ashok Chakras and Shaurya Chakras for operations including the 26/11 Mumbai attack response (2008).

The Indian Coast Guard is organised into Regional Headquarters (West, East, North-East, Andaman & Nicobar) and District Headquarters, operating surface vessels, hovercraft, fixed-wing aircraft, and helicopters for maritime patrol and enforcement.

Role
  • Search and rescue operations during cyclones, ship distress calls, and maritime accidents
  • Anti-smuggling, anti-piracy, and coastal/EEZ law enforcement
  • Marine pollution response and protection of marine ecology
  • Coastal security coordination with Navy, Marine Police, and customs after 26/11
Awards

Coast Guard personnel have been honoured with Tatrakshak Medals (the Coast Guard's own gallantry and distinguished service awards), along with President's and Director General's Commendation medals for outstanding rescue operations and anti-smuggling actions over the decades.

The Elite Few

Special Forces & Commando Units

Every major branch of India's security apparatus — Army, Navy, Air Force, Coast Guard, and the Central Armed Police Forces — maintains its own specially trained commando element for high-risk, high-skill operations. Here's who they are and what they actually do.

  • Para (Special Forces) — The elite SF battalions of the Parachute Regiment (1, 2, 3, 4, 9, 10, 11, 12, 21 Para SF) trained for deep-penetration raids, sabotage, hostage rescue, and unconventional warfare behind enemy lines. Selection involves a gruelling probation period with a very high attrition rate. The Parachute Regiment overall fields ~15 battalions (9 Para SF + 5 Airborne + 1 Counter-Insurgency / Rashtriya Rifles).
  • Para (Airborne) — 5, 6, 7, 23, 29 Para battalions of the Parachute Regiment, trained for mass parachute insertion and rapid airborne assault as part of the 50 (Independent) Parachute Brigade.
  • Ghatak Platoons — Every infantry battalion in the Army maintains its own internal commando platoon (Ghatak — "killer"), trained for spearheading assaults, raids, and high-risk reconnaissance ahead of the main force — the "first in" element at unit level.
  • Bhairav Light Commando Battalions — Newly raised (2025) compact 250-personnel shock units evolved from the Ghatak concept, designed for clandestine cross-border surprise strikes.
  • Special Frontier Force (SFF) / Vikas Battalion / Establishment 22 — A covert paramilitary force under the Cabinet Secretariat (R&AW), recruited largely from Tibetan refugees, trained for high-altitude covert operations along the northern borders. Played a key role in the 1971 war and the 2020 Ladakh stand-off.
  • Special Group (SG) — An Army-affiliated covert unit for clandestine operations, separate from the SFF, used for sensitive cross-border and counter-terror tasks.
  • Force Commander Reconnaissance Group — A pathfinder element historically used for clandestine surveillance and target reconnaissance ahead of Special Forces operations.

MARCOS (officially the Marine Commando Force, raised 1987) is the Indian Navy's special operations unit, trained for amphibious assault, ship and oil-rig hijack response, underwater demolition, riverine and coastal counter-terrorism, and maritime reconnaissance. MARCOS commandos are qualified combat divers and parachutists, and the unit has played a role in coastal security operations and joint counter-terror responses, including support during the 2008 Mumbai attacks.

The Garud Commando Force (raised 2004) is the IAF's special forces unit, responsible for the rapid deployment defence of forward airbases, combat search-and-rescue of downed aircrew in hostile territory, and direct-action tasks in coordination with Army and Navy special forces. Garud commandos are trained in close-quarter battle, free-fall parachuting, and demolition.

The Indian Coast Guard does not maintain a separate "commando" force of its own; instead, it relies on specially trained boarding and interdiction teams aboard its fast patrol vessels for anti-smuggling, anti-piracy, and law-enforcement boardings at sea. For larger counter-terrorism contingencies along the coast, it operates jointly with Navy MARCOS and state Marine Police units as part of India's layered coastal security grid set up after the 2008 Mumbai attacks.

The National Security Guard (NSG), nicknamed the "Black Cats", is India's premier federal counter-terrorism and counter-hijacking force. It was raised in 1984 (after Operation Blue Star) under the Ministry of Home Affairs and is modelled broadly on Germany's GSG-9 and Britain's SAS. NSG is deployed only on the orders of the central government for high-stakes contingencies — hostage rescue, hijack response, VIP protection, and bomb disposal.

Structure
  • Special Action Group (SAG) — The offensive arm, drawn entirely from the Indian Army, tasked with direct-action assaults, hostage rescue, and counter-terror strikes.
  • Special Ranger Group (SRG) — Drawn from Central Armed Police Forces and state police, responsible for VIP/VVIP protection and supporting SAG in operations.
  • National Bomb Data Centre (NBDC) — NSG's specialist bomb-disposal and explosives-intelligence wing.
  • Regional Hubs — NSG has hubs at Mumbai, Chennai, Kolkata, Hyderabad, and Gandhinagar to cut response time after the 26/11 Mumbai attacks.
Notable Operations

Operation Black Thunder (1988, Golden Temple), Operation Vajra Shakti / Operation Black Tornado (2008, 26/11 Mumbai attacks), Pathankot airbase attack response (2016), and numerous hijack/hostage interventions. The NSG headquarters is at Manesar, Haryana.

The Commando Battalion for Resolute Action (CoBRA) is the specialised jungle-warfare and guerrilla unit of the Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF), raised in 2008 specifically to counter Left-Wing Extremism (Maoist / Naxal insurgency) in central and eastern India. CoBRA is the only CAPF unit raised exclusively for guerrilla and jungle warfare.

Role & Training
  • Deep-jungle patrolling, ambush and counter-ambush in dense forest terrain (Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh).
  • Intelligence-led small-team operations, tracking, and rescue of abducted personnel.
  • Trained at the CoBRA training school and in joint courses with the Army's jungle warfare schools (Vairengte).
  • Currently fielding 10 battalions spread across the Naxal-affected "Red Corridor".

CoBRA personnel have been awarded multiple Shaurya Chakras, Kirti Chakras, and Police Medals for Gallantry for actions in the anti-Naxal grid.

The Special Protection Group (SPG) is an armed force of the Government of India under the Cabinet Secretariat, raised in 1985 after the assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, and given statutory backing by the SPG Act, 1988. Its sole mandate is the proximate security of the Prime Minister of India (and, after the 2019 amendment, the PM's immediate family residing with the PM at the official residence).

SPG personnel are drawn on deputation from the CAPF and state police, and undergo intensive training in close-protection drills, advanced driving, marksmanship, and counter-assault tactics. SPG's signature elements include the Black Cat-style assault uniform of the Close Protection Team (CPT) and the layered "ring of steel" around the protectee.

  • BSF Special Operations Group (SOG) / Counter Insurgency & Anti-Terrorist (CIAT) Units — Trained for anti-infiltration ambushes and counter-terrorist operations along the western (Pakistan) and eastern (Bangladesh) borders. BSF also fields specialist Water Wing and Camel Contingent for Rann of Kutch / Rajasthan desert sectors.
  • ITBP Special Frontier Task Force & Mountaineering / Rescue Teams — Trained for high-altitude combat, glacier warfare, and disaster-response rescue operations in the Himalayas; ITBP guards the entire India-China LAC from Karakoram Pass to Jachep La.
  • SSB Special Operations Battalion (SOB) — Focused on intelligence-led counter-insurgency operations along the open Nepal and Bhutan borders.
  • CISF Quick Reaction Teams (QRTs) & Special Security Group (SSG) — Rapid-response armed teams stationed at airports, metro networks, nuclear/space installations, and critical infrastructure for immediate response to security threats.
  • Assam Rifles Counter-Insurgency Units — The "Sentinels of the North-East" maintain dedicated counter-insurgency columns and the Assam Rifles Training Centre & School (Dimapur) is one of India's premier CI/JW training institutions.
  • RPF Commando Battalion for Railway Security (CORAS) — Raised 2019; specialist commandos for hostage and high-threat situations on the Indian Railways network.

Several states maintain their own commando units — modelled broadly on NSG — for rapid counter-terror and anti-insurgency response within state jurisdiction. The most prominent are:

  • Force One (Maharashtra) — Raised after the 26/11 Mumbai attacks (2009); rapid counter-terror response unit of Maharashtra Police, designed to neutralise threats before NSG can deploy.
  • Greyhounds (Andhra Pradesh / Telangana) — Raised 1989; widely regarded as India's most effective anti-Naxal force, specialising in deep-jungle small-team operations.
  • OCTOPUS — Organisation for Counter Terrorist Operations (Telangana) — Telangana State Police's elite counter-terror unit.
  • Thunderbolts (Kerala) — Kerala Police's elite commando force for counter-terror, anti-Maoist, and VIP protection duties.
  • SOG & Special Task Force (Jammu & Kashmir Police) — Frontline counter-terror unit deeply involved in anti-militancy operations in the Kashmir Valley.
  • C-60 Commandos (Maharashtra — Gadchiroli) — Specialist anti-Naxal force of Maharashtra Police operating in the Gadchiroli–Chandrapur forests.
  • STF / SOG (Uttar Pradesh, West Bengal, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand etc.) — Each state maintains its own Special Task Force / Special Operations Group for organised-crime, counter-terror, and anti-Naxal operations.
  • ATS — Anti-Terrorism Squad — Investigative-cum-operational counter-terror units maintained by Maharashtra, Gujarat, Delhi, UP, Karnataka and other states.

Note: State police commandos are not Central Armed Police Forces, but they are commonly asked about in current-affairs and general-awareness sections of defence exams.

The Final Frontier

SSB Interview

The Services Selection Board (SSB) is a 5-day personality and intelligence assessment. It evaluates Officer-Like Qualities (OLQs) across three dimensions.

DAY 1

Screening Tests

OIR Test

Officer Intelligence Rating — Two sets of verbal and non-verbal reasoning questions. Scores candidates on intelligence rating IR1 to IR5. Used for initial screening.

PPDT

Picture Perception & Description Test — A hazy image is shown for 30 seconds. Candidates write a story (60 words), then discuss in groups. Tests perception, communication, and leadership.

Approximately 50–70% of candidates are screened out after Day 1.
DAY 2–3

Psychological Tests

TAT — Thematic Apperception Test

11 pictures + 1 blank slide. Write a story for each (4 minutes per picture). Stories reveal subconscious personality traits, values, and thinking patterns. Avoid negative themes — heroes must overcome challenges.

WAT — Word Association Test

60 words shown one at a time (15 seconds each). Write the first sentence that comes to mind. Tests spontaneity, positivity, and subconscious association patterns.

SRT — Situation Reaction Test

60 situations in 30 minutes. Respond with the first action that comes to mind. Reveals decision-making, common sense, social sensitivity, and initiative.

SD — Self Description

Write what your Parents, Teachers, Friends, and YOU think about you. Consistency with behavior observed during GTO and Interview is critical.

DAY 3–4

GTO Tasks

Group Discussion (GD)

2 rounds — Current Affairs & Abstract topic. Contribute meaningful points, encourage others to speak, avoid dominating or going silent.

Group Planning Exercise (GPE)

Analyze a crisis map, plan solutions individually, then discuss as a group. Tests analytical thinking and communication.

Progressive Group Task (PGT)

Group crosses 4 obstacles using limited materials (plank, rope, barrel). Cooperation, leadership, and initiative are observed.

Half Group Task (HGT)

Same as PGT but with a smaller group (5–6). Each person's contribution is more visible.

Individual Obstacles

10 obstacles, each with a point value (1–10). You have 3 minutes to score maximum points. Tests individual courage, speed, and technique.

Command Task

You lead 2–3 subordinates to solve a task. The GTO assigns subordinates based on observed performance. Pure leadership test.

Lecturette

3-minute talk on a topic chosen from 4 slips (1 minute preparation). Tests knowledge, communication, and confidence under pressure.

Final Group Task (FGT)

Preparation Guide

How to Crack SSB — Test-by-Test

Actionable strategies for every stage of the 5-day board

What is Tested

Officer Intelligence Rating (OIR) has two booklets — Verbal (analogies, synonyms, series, coding-decoding, sentence arrangement) and Non-Verbal (figure series, mirror images, pattern completion, odd-one-out, cube folding). Both are MCQ, strictly time-bound.

Target Score

Aim for IR-1 or IR-2 (top bands). Candidates below IR-4 rarely get screened in. Speed + accuracy both matter.

Practice Plan
  • Solve RS Aggarwal Verbal & Non-Verbal Reasoning — minimum 2 chapters daily
  • Practice figure series, mirror images, cube nets — these repeat in patterns
  • Timed practice: 40 questions in 17 minutes (simulate actual speed)
  • Coding-decoding and number series — high frequency topics
  • Apps: SSB OIR Practice, Reasoning Master
💡 Key Insight: OIR is not about knowledge — it's about processing speed. Practice daily for 30 days to see visible improvement.
What Happens

A hazy image is projected for 30 seconds. You then write a story (60 words, 4 minutes) covering: number of characters, age, mood, and what is happening. After writing, your group discusses and arrives at a common story in 15 minutes.

Story Formula
  • Background: What led to this situation
  • Action: What the hero is doing right now
  • Result: Positive outcome achieved
  • Hero must be active, resourceful, and successful
Discussion Strategy
  • Speak first or second — present your story clearly in 30 seconds
  • Listen actively — incorporate others' good points into the common story
  • Never argue or interrupt; disagree politely
  • Help the group converge — suggest a combined storyline
  • Maintain eye contact with group, not the assessor
✓ DO
  • Keep hero positive and proactive
  • Stick to 1 clear storyline
  • Encourage quiet members to speak
✗ DON'T
  • Write a negative ending
  • Dominate the group discussion
  • Change your story drastically to please others
Structure

11 pictures + 1 blank slide. Each picture shown for 30 seconds, then 4 minutes to write a story. The blank slide: write any story you want. Total time ~55 minutes.

Story Structure (4 Ws)
  • What led to the situation? (background)
  • What is happening now? (action)
  • What is the hero thinking/feeling?
  • What will happen next? (always positive outcome)
💡 Use one main character only — make them resourceful, decisive, and successful. The psychologist looks for pattern across all 11 stories.
Themes to Incorporate
  • Problem-solving and overcoming challenges
  • Leadership, teamwork, responsibility
  • Community service, national pride
  • Ethical decisions under pressure
  • Sports, adventure, technical achievement
✓ DO
  • Vary story themes across slides
  • Keep hero active, not passive
  • End every story positively
✗ DON'T
  • Write tragic/negative endings
  • Repeat same theme 11 times
  • Write vague or ambiguous stories
Structure

60 words shown one at a time on screen — 15 seconds each to write the first sentence that comes to mind. Total: 15 minutes. Words are ordinary (e.g. RIVER, MOTHER, FAILURE, JUNGLE, FRIEND).

What the Psychologist Sees

Patterns across all 60 responses reveal your subconscious attitude — optimism, social awareness, confidence, negativity, aggression. Responses must be sentences, not single words.

Response Framework
  • Write a complete sentence — Subject + Verb + Object
  • Keep it positive, action-oriented, and relevant
  • Relate to leadership, service, or achievement when natural
  • Don't overthink — first positive thought is best

Examples:

FAILURE → "Failure is a stepping stone to success if one learns from it."

JUNGLE → "He led his team through the jungle with confidence and calm."

ENEMY → "Soldiers guard the border against the enemy with full dedication."

💡 Practice daily: pick 10 random words, set a 15-second timer for each, write one sentence. Do this for 3 weeks.
Structure

60 situations in a booklet — 30 minutes to respond (30 seconds per situation). Write the first practical action you would take. Situations are everyday social, emergency, and moral dilemmas.

Sample Situations
  • "You see a road accident on your way to an important exam. You…"
  • "A junior colleague makes a serious error in a report. You…"
  • "You are lost in a forest with no phone signal. You…"
  • "Your friend asks you to lie to cover his mistake. You…"
Response Principles
  • Always take action — don't write "I will think about it"
  • Show initiative, social responsibility, and courage
  • Practical & common-sense response — not dramatic
  • Keep responses short (1–2 lines)
  • Don't abandon responsibility to help only yourself
✓ DO
  • Help others first if life is at risk
  • Show ethical decision-making
  • Write confidently and quickly
✗ DON'T
  • Write selfish responses
  • Leave situations unanswered
  • Over-explain — brevity wins
What You Write

In ~15 minutes, write what 5 people think about you:

  • Parents — What do they say about you?
  • Teachers — Your academic & personal traits
  • Friends — Social, fun, reliable aspects
  • You yourself — Honest self-assessment
  • What you want to improve — 1–2 real weaknesses
Critical Rules
  • Consistency is everything — SD must match your PIQ, GTO behavior, and Interview answers
  • Mention real, specific qualities — avoid generic words like "good, hardworking"
  • Weakness must be genuine but not fatal — e.g. "I get impatient when work is delayed" (not "I lose my temper")
  • Write in third person: "His friends say he is…"
💡 Write your SD draft at home and review it against your actual behavior. The IO will cross-check SD against your Interview responses on Day 4.

Military History

Wars & Operations

India's major wars, border conflicts, and named military operations from 1947 to the present — high-frequency topics for SSB Lecturette, GK papers & Group Discussions.

First Kashmir War (1947–48)
UN Ceasefire

India vs Pakistan — over the accession of Jammu & Kashmir

Began after tribal raiders backed by Pakistan entered J&K following the Maharaja's accession to India. Ended with a UN-mediated ceasefire on 1 January 1949, establishing the Ceasefire Line (later the Line of Control).

Sino-Indian War (1962)
Chinese Gains

India vs China — border dispute in Aksai Chin and the North-East

Fought across the Ladakh and NEFA (now Arunachal Pradesh) sectors over disputed Himalayan borders. China declared a unilateral ceasefire on 21 November 1962 after gaining ground in Aksai Chin; led to major reforms in India's defence preparedness.

Indo-Pak War (1965)
UN Ceasefire

India vs Pakistan — triggered by Pakistan's Operation Gibraltar

Pakistan infiltrated forces into J&K hoping to spark an uprising; large tank battles took place in the Punjab/Sialkot sector, including the Battle of Asal Uttar. Ended via the Tashkent Declaration in January 1966.

Indo-Pak War / Bangladesh Liberation War (1971)
Indian Victory

India vs Pakistan — led to the creation of Bangladesh

India intervened in support of the Bangladesh liberation movement in former East Pakistan. The Indian Navy's Operation Trident & Operation Python struck Karachi harbour, while ground forces advanced on both fronts. Ended in 13 days with the surrender of over 90,000 Pakistani troops at Dhaka — the largest military surrender since World War II.

Kargil War (1999)
Indian Victory

India vs Pakistan — high-altitude conflict in the Kargil sector, J&K

Pakistani soldiers and militants occupied vacated high-altitude posts in winter. India launched Operation Vijay (ground) and Operation Safed Sagar (air) to evict them. Indian forces recaptured all key peaks, including Tiger Hill, by July 1999.

Nathu La & Cho La Clashes (1967)
Indian Hold

India vs China — Sikkim border, post-1962 tensions

Brief but intense artillery and infantry clashes along the Sikkim border. Indian troops held their positions and inflicted significant casualties, helping restore military confidence after 1962.

Sumdorong Chu Standoff (1986–87)
De-escalated

India vs China — Arunachal Pradesh border

A tense military standoff after Chinese troops set up positions in the disputed Sumdorong Chu valley. India responded with Operation Falcon, airlifting troops to the area. Resolved through diplomatic talks without major fighting.

Siachen Conflict (1984–present)
Indian Control

India vs Pakistan — world's highest battlefield, eastern Karakoram

India launched Operation Meghdoot in April 1984 to occupy key passes ahead of Pakistan, gaining control of the Saltoro Ridge. Sporadic skirmishes followed through the 1980s–90s; India has maintained control of the glacier since.

Galwan Valley Clash (2020)
Disengagement

India vs China — Eastern Ladakh, Line of Actual Control

A violent hand-to-hand clash broke out on 15 June 2020 amid a broader standoff along the LAC, with casualties on both sides. Followed by multiple rounds of military and diplomatic talks leading to phased disengagement from friction points.

Operation Polo (1948)
Annexation

Police action to integrate the princely state of Hyderabad into India

A five-day military action in September 1948 that ended the rule of the Nizam of Hyderabad and brought the state into the Indian Union.

Operation Vijay — Goa (1961)
Annexation

Military action to end Portuguese colonial rule in Goa, Daman & Diu

A swift 36-hour operation in December 1961 by the Army, Navy & Air Force that ended over 450 years of Portuguese rule on Indian soil.

Operation Meghdoot (1984)
Indian Control

Pre-emptive occupation of the Siachen Glacier

India airlifted troops onto the Saltoro Ridge just ahead of a planned Pakistani move, securing control of the glacier that continues to this day.

Operation Blue Star (1984)
Domestic Operation

Action to remove armed militants from the Golden Temple complex, Amritsar

A controversial domestic security operation in June 1984 that resulted in significant casualties and remains a sensitive chapter in India's internal security history.

Operation Pawan (1987–90)
Withdrawal

Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF) deployment in Sri Lanka

Indian troops were deployed to disarm Tamil militant groups under the 1987 Indo-Sri Lanka Accord. The mission faced prolonged guerrilla resistance and was withdrawn in 1990.

Operation Cactus (1988)
Successful Intervention

Rapid intervention to foil a coup attempt in the Maldives

Indian paratroopers were airlifted overnight to Malé at the Maldivian government's request, restoring order within hours and with no Indian casualties.

Operation Parakram (2001–02)
Mobilisation, No War

Large-scale military mobilisation along the Pakistan border

Launched after the December 2001 Parliament attack, this was the largest mobilisation since 1971. Forces remained deployed for nearly a year before stand-down, without a full-scale war breaking out.

Operation Sindoor (2025)
Strikes Conducted

Precision strikes on terror infrastructure in Pakistan & PoK

Launched in the early hours of 7 May 2025 in response to the 22 April Pahalgam terror attack. Indian forces struck multiple terror-linked sites; this triggered four days of cross-border strikes, drone exchanges and shelling before a ceasefire was reached on 10 May 2025.

Operation Rakshak (1990–present)
Ongoing

Long-running counter-insurgency operation in Jammu & Kashmir

An umbrella operation by the Army and central forces to counter militancy and secure the Kashmir Valley, running continuously since the early 1990s.

Operation Black Tornado (2008)
Hostages Freed

NSG counter-terror operation during the 26/11 Mumbai attacks

National Security Guard commandos cleared the Taj Mahal Palace, Oberoi Trident and Nariman House over three days, ending the siege and freeing hostages.

Operation All Out (2017–present)
Ongoing

Intensified anti-militancy operations across the Kashmir Valley

A coordinated Army-CRPF-J&K Police drive launched in 2017 to systematically target militant networks operating in the Valley.

2016 Surgical Strikes
Strikes Conducted

Cross-LoC strikes on militant launch pads after the Uri attack

Indian Army special forces crossed the Line of Control on the night of 28–29 September 2016, targeting launch pads used for infiltration, in response to the attack on an Army base in Uri.

Balakot Air Strike (2019)
Strikes Conducted

Air strike on a terror training camp in Pakistan after the Pulwama attack

IAF Mirage 2000 jets struck an alleged Jaish-e-Mohammed training facility near Balakot on 26 February 2019, days after the Pulwama bombing that killed 40 CRPF personnel. A subsequent aerial engagement led to the brief capture and return of an IAF pilot.

UN Peacekeeping Missions
Ongoing Commitment

Indian Armed Forces contribution to United Nations peacekeeping

India is among the largest historical contributors of troops to UN peacekeeping missions worldwide, with deployments across Africa, the Middle East and elsewhere over several decades.
